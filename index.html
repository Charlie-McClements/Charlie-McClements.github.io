Fixing Leaking Windows

In this blog we will be following along how I resealed the windows on my Etap 28i. Which products we used and where to find them, and things that didn't work so great so as you can avoid them and save yourself some money!

Before

After

The issues with the window shown above weren't something we could ignore. The previous owner had actually suggested going to the point of just fibreglassing the hole over to seal it as he had given up trying to make it watertight. Determined not to loose a large source of natural light in the material I thought it would be worth giving it a go.

We started by taking the old window off. As ever this was easier said than done. The leaking was so bad that alot of the poor quality stainless steel bolts that hold the window down had corroded to the point that the nut was never going to come off but yet not corroded enough that it could simply be snapped off. It was time for the grinder to come out.

Using the angle grinder to deepen the grooves in the cross head bolts made the metal thin enough that with the help of a chisel and hammer the head could be snapped off the shaft, allowing the shaft to drop out the whole and the window to pop off.

With the window off, and a nice big hole in our boat, it was cleaning time. Not much to it other than a good scraper to get rid of old useless silicone and a sponge and water for the rest.

The next step of the process was to apply the foam sealing strips. We chose to use foam as the silicone, as evident by the old leaks, doesn't seem to last very long. It is also extremely messy to work with and doesnt deal very well with uneven surfaces. Something which things I repair tend to be. Therefore we chose to go with a foam sealing strip. The layout of the strips is important to prevent leaks at the corners. The diagram shows the layout we suggest to minimise any potential water ingress.

But what foam should you use? We used closed cell neoprene foam for it's waterproof properties as well as it's durability and its softness. A more rigid foam won't conform to the shape of the hull as easily. The adhesive on the foam doesn't have to be anything special as it is only to hold the foam in place until the window is bolted back down. The link to the foam we purchased is here. We used 10mm thick foam however, if your hull is very smooth and even you'd likely get away with less.

At this stage we made a mistake. We chose to simply bolt the window into position without using any washers on the outside face of the bolt. The thory being that they'd sink into the plastic window a little forming a water tight seal. Wishful thinking... All of the bolts began to leak in the next heavy rainfall resulting in us putting some ugly sealant around the outside of the bolt heads. Although this worked I'm not sure how long it will last and it's far from a pretty job. Therefore we recommend that you make use of some rubber washers on the bolt heads in order to make a much better seal against the elements. The link for which can be found here.
